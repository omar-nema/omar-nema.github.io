TOOL STORAGE

Adaptable, visible, dynamic

STROL-120 is an integrated tool and hardware storage system that I designed, prototyped and constructed over the course of a semester with three RISD students. The system is designed for use at the recently-opened Brown Design Workshop.

The STROL-120 system has fixed and moving elements, a unique geometry to maximize storage space, interchangeable storage units, color-coding, and full self-assembly. 

Materials: plywood, 80/20, aluminum sheet, hardware, vinyl

Fall 2014

 

 

 

 

 

 


120°

Moving elements in STROL-120 enable students to work on projects without having to lug around tools, and fixed elements ensure that certain tools are available for use at all times. Additionally, the 120 degree angle (Y-shape) in our moving unit allows multiple groups/students to work on the same cart.

This design breaks up the space in the crammed Brown Design Workshop -- rather than working near one tool wall, students can instead spread out, and distribute space more efficiently. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


Adaptability

From the ground-up, we designed our system to be flexible. 

We provided our users (managers of the Brown Design Workshop) with a booklet for assembling our STROL-120 units (no power tools required, just some elbow grease and a wrench). 

All tool-holders, and structural support pieces (apart from prefabricated 80/20) were laser cut. Missing pieces, or small adjustments to pre-existing components can simply be remedied using our pre-made files. 

 


 


Technical details

Our system relies on a skeleton of 80/20 (left) for structural support, ease of assembly and to enable the attachment of tool-holders.

A series of y-shaped laser cut (right) aluminum centerpieces attach to the 80/20 ribs, binding the three walls of the cart together.

 

 

 

 

 

Tool holders, designed in SolidWorks and laser cut from sheet aluminum, are attached with thumb screws through slots in each panel that align with the 80/20 skeleton inside. Hand-tightening the screw fixes the holder securely to the face of panel,allowing the user to easily replace tool holders or alter the arrangement of tools.

 

 


 

 

 

 


Model-making

First prototypes. Concrete, OSB, and cardboard used.

We experimented with different forms and shapes. The design to the left offered much more room for multiple users and groups to interact with the same cart, so we built on this in our final iteration.

 

 

 

 

 

 



Ideation

Sketch, sketch, build.

We explored a number of ideas on paper, and used rapid prototyping methods to experiment with different solutions

(some highlights: a tool inventory website, to a personal, tray-based tool storage system, workstations integrated with tool storage)

 

 

 

 

 

 


Research

Top left: System (or lack of?) for tool storage prior to STROL-120.

Top right, and bottom left: Experimented with different tool shapes and sizes to create optimal arrangement. Ultimately decided to leave tool arrangement up to user, as this allowed for future adaptability (if space changes or acquires more tools).

Field research (observing students work in the space through time-lapse videos) led us to conclude that mobile and fixed components are needed to allow for different scopes of projects. Since the Brown Design Workshop is still being built, adaptability is crucial to ensure that the project stays relevant and useful.
